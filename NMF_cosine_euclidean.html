The Geometry of NMF Vectors

Why Cosine Similarity collapses non-negative sparse vectors, and why Euclidean Distance preserves the manifold.

The Euclidean Manifold

Euclidean distance measures the true spatial distance between points. It respects both the direction (flavor of work) and the magnitude (intensity/seniority of the work).

  • Junior and Senior Engineers are separated by their magnitude (intensity of the components).
  • The manifold is healthy, distributed, and thresholding (e.g., cutoff: 0.95) effectively filters out mismatched seniorities.

The Cosine Collapse

Cosine similarity normalizes all vectors to a unit circle. It only cares about the angle between points, entirely destroying magnitude.

  • NMF vectors are completely non-negative, trapping them in this single 90° quadrant.
  • Because NMF forces sparsity, vectors hug the axes.
  • Look at the Engineers: The Junior, Senior, and Principal all collapse onto the exact same coordinate. They all score 0.99+ similarity.

NMF outputs are strictly non-negative (no negative numbers). This means vectors cannot point in opposite directions (180° apart). The maximum angular difference is only 90°. This extreme confinement makes Cosine highly susceptible to collapsing sparse data.
